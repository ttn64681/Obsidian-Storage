## Comparisons:
- efficiency:
	- rasterization is faster for real-time apps (like video games) b/c it processes only visible surface
	- ray tracing, while computationally intensive, can produce more realistic images, especially w/ reflections, refractions, and global illumination
- visibility:
	- rasterization is inherently a "what's in front" algorithm
	- ray tracing is a "what's visible algorithm"
- complexity:
	- rasterization focuses on polygons (primarily triangles) and their projection onto 2d space
	- ray tracing deals w/ rays and their intersections w/ scene objects, including reflections, refractions, and shadows
- gpus:
	- currently, are optimized for rasterization, although w/ technological advancements, ...

# World Coordinates
- objects: in world coordinates, every object's position is defined in relation to a global, fixed reference point (often called the **origin**)
	- It's like placing diff props on a stage
- lights: light sources (not shown in image) are also placed in the world coords
	- Their position and direction (if they're directional lights) determine how they will illuminate the scene.
- camera (or Eye): Represents the viewpoint from which the scene will be rendered.
	- In world coords, we determine
		- where camera is placed
		- where it's looking, and
		- which direction is "up" for the camera
	- Recall camera placement in three.js
- **Provides a consistent, unified space where entire scene is composed before further processing**

# 1. View(ing) Transformation (Camera Transformation)
- **Adjust the scene based on camera's position and direction**
	- Transform the world to camera coordinates.
- **The Camera in 3D Graphics:**
	- The camera remains fixed; the world moves.

- **Camera Position:** at the origin
- **Camera Orientation:**
	- +z-axis towards camera, lens facing -z.
	- x-axis aims to right of camera, and the y-axis aims up
- **Clipping Planes:**
	- Far: Farthest dist from camera and objects are still rendered
	- Near: Closest distance from camera and objects are still rendered

## Clipping Planes & Field of View (FOV)
- **def:** the extent of the observable world seen at any given moment
- **vertical vs horizontal:** fov can refer to vertical angle, the horizontal angle, or both
	- Vertical FOV: the angle of upwards and downwards view ℗imarily used by openGL)
	- Horizontal FOV: depends on the vertical AGOV and the aspect ratio
- **Role in 3D Graphics:**
	- Determines how "zoomed in" or "zoomed out" a scene appears.
	- Affects the sense of depth and scale in the scene
- **Physical Camera:**
	- wider FOV, wider lens, shorter focal length (35mm)

# 2. Projection Transformation
- Map the specified view volume (defined by near/far clipping planes and the shape of the frustum) to the canonical view volume.
- Canonical View Volume are set in a normalized device coordinates (NDC) space
	- a cube where coordinates range from -1 to 1 on each axis.
